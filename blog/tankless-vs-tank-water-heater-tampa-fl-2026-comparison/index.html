What Is the Difference Between Tankless and Tank Water Heaters?

A water heater is the appliance that heats potable water for showers, dishwashing, and laundry.

Tank heaters store and continuously heat 40–80 gallons of water; tankless units heat water on demand as it flows through the unit.

A tank water heater (a storage cylinder, typically 40–80 gallons, that keeps water hot 24/7) uses standby energy to maintain temperature. A tankless water heater (also called an on-demand unit, which heats water only when a tap is opened) uses a high-output burner or electric coil to raise water temperature 70°F+ within seconds. The team at test1 (a plumbing business in Tampa, FL) sees both styles installed across South Tampa, Carrollwood, and Brandon neighborhoods.

"Demand-type (tankless) water heaters can be 24% to 34% more energy efficient than conventional storage tank water heaters for homes that use 41 gallons or less of hot water daily."U.S. Department of Energy — energy.gov

Why Does Tampa's Hard Water Affect Water Heater Choice?

Hard water is water with elevated calcium and magnesium content that leaves mineral scale on heating components.

Tampa's 7–9 grain-per-gallon hardness shortens tankless heater life by 30–40% if no softening or annual descaling is performed.

Tankless heat exchangers have narrow water passages. Scale buildup chokes flow and triggers error codes within 2–4 years on unsoftened systems. Tank heaters tolerate scale better but trade that for sediment that bakes onto the bottom of the tank, reducing efficiency 8–12% per year. Per Florida Statute 553.842, all water heater replacements require a permit and inspection by the local building department — homeowners in unincorporated Hillsborough County can verify at the Hillsborough County Building Services portal.

Gas Tankless vs. Electric Tankless

Gas tankless vs. electric tankless: gas tankless is better for whole-home use because a single unit can deliver 6–9 gallons per minute. Electric tankless is a tradeoff because most residential 200-amp panels only support point-of-use models at 1.5–3 GPM without expensive service upgrades.

A common Tampa scenario

A typical pattern in Tampa neighborhoods like Westchase or New Tampa: a family of four in a 2,200-square-foot home installs a 50-gallon gas tank heater when building in 2010. By 2026, the tank is leaking from the bottom — a classic 12–15 year failure point. They face a choice: replace with the same tank style for $1,800 and have hot water restored in one day, or upgrade to gas tankless for $3,800, requiring gas line resizing from 1/2" to 3/4" and a new stainless vent through the roof. The tankless option pays back its premium in roughly 9–11 years through energy savings but adds resale appeal in a market where buyers near Bayshore Boulevard increasingly expect modern fixtures.

How Long Does Water Heater Installation Take?

Installation time is the labor window from old-unit shutoff to new-unit commissioning.

Tank-for-tank swaps typically take 3–5 hours; tankless conversions take 6–10 hours including venting and gas/electrical upgrades.

Typical installation sequence

  1. Step 1: Load calculation — A licensed plumber measures peak GPM demand based on fixture count and household size.
  2. Step 2: Permit pull — Florida Building Code 2023 requires a mechanical permit through the local jurisdiction before any work begins.
  3. Step 3: Removal and disposal — The old unit is drained, disconnected, and hauled off per Hillsborough County waste rules.
  4. Step 4: Infrastructure prep — Gas lines, vents, condensate drains, or 240V circuits are upgraded to match the new unit's specs.
  5. Step 5: Installation and pressure test — The unit is mounted, connected, and tested at line pressure for leaks.
  6. Step 6: Inspection — A county inspector verifies code compliance before the homeowner uses the system.

In Florida, water heater installation must be done by a Certified or Registered Plumbing Contractor licensed by the state.

What to verify before hiring

  • State license: A Certified Plumbing Contractor (CFC#) or Registered Plumbing Contractor (RF#) license through the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation.
  • Insurance: Florida requires $100,000 general liability and $25,000 property damage minimum for plumbing contractors.
  • Workers' compensation: Required for any contractor with employees per Florida Statute 440.
  • Manufacturer training: Tankless brands like Rinnai and Navien require factory certification for warranty validity.

8-step verification checklist before signing

  1. Verify the CFC or RF license number at MyFloridaLicense.com.
  2. Request a current certificate of insurance with your name as certificate holder.
  3. Confirm a permit will be pulled — not "permit-by-homeowner."
  4. Get the manufacturer model number in writing before signing.
  5. Ask about warranty: 6 years on tank, 12–15 years on tankless heat exchangers is standard.
  6. Confirm haul-away and disposal are included.
  7. Request a written scope including gas line size, vent type, and condensate handling.
  8. Verify inspection is included in the quoted price.

#Red flags to watch for

  • Refuses to pull a permit or asks you to pull it yourself.
  • No certificate of insurance available on request.
  • Unmarked vehicles with no business signage or visible license number.
  • Quote is verbal only — no written scope or model numbers.

Myths vs. facts

Myth: Tankless water heaters provide unlimited instant hot water.

Fact: They provide continuous hot water but at a flow-limited GPM rate; running two showers may exceed capacity.
